Welcome to Women's Lacrosse

Introduction

Discover the exciting world of women's lacrosse! From its rich history to the latest stars and rules of the game, explore all about this fast-paced sport that is joining the 2028 Olympics.

Women's lacrosse has rapidly grown into one of the most exciting and fast-paced team sports, with roots tracing back to Native American traditions. It combines speed, skill, and strategy, making it both thrilling to play and to watch. Over the past few decades, the sport has witnessed an increase in participation at both youth and collegiate levels. According to the NCAA, women's lacrosse is now one of the fastest-growing sports in the U.S., with over 40,000 participants across all divisions.

Internationally, women's lacrosse has expanded significantly, gaining popularity in Canada, England, Australia, and Japan. The establishment of the Women's Lacrosse World Cup, governed by World Lacrosse, has further solidified the sport's presence on the global stage, with over 30 countries now competing. This rise in international participation has been crucial in securing lacrosse's return to the Olympic Games, where it will be featured in the 2028 Los Angeles Olympics. The inclusion of lacrosse in the Olympics marks a historic moment for the sport, with the potential to inspire a new generation of athletes worldwide.

Charlotte North - USA

Current women's world number 1

Charlotte North is a record-breaking American lacrosse star known for her outstanding attacking skills.

After graduating from Boston College in 2023, she led Team USA with 23 goals in the 2022 World Lacrosse Championships, tying for second with 28 points overall.

As the first pick in the 2022 Athletes Unlimited Draft, North quickly became a league leader in goals and points, amassing 1,061 leaderboard points and setting a record with six goals in the finale.

In 2023, she continued her success, ranking 17th in the league with 32 goals, 44 points, and 180 MVP points, matching the record for six goals in a single game.

At the collegiate level, North ranks second all-time in NCAA Division I history with 358 career goals and remains the only player with three 90+ goal seasons, winning numerous accolades, including the Tewaaraton Award.

History of Lacrosse

Lacrosse has a rich history that dates back to the indigenous peoples of North America. Originating over 500 years ago, the sport was played by Native American tribes, primarily in the northeastern United States and Canada. It served not only as a game but also as a means of resolving conflicts, training warriors, and fostering community spirit. Early versions of lacrosse featured teams of varying sizes and were played on expansive fields, sometimes involving hundreds of players. The game was known by various names among different tribes, with the term "lacrosse" deriving from the French word for "the stick," a reference to the equipment used in the game.

In the 19th century, lacrosse began to evolve and gain popularity outside of Native American communities. The sport was introduced to European settlers, who adapted the rules and structure of the game. In 1867, the first formalized rules were established by the Montreal Lacrosse Club, marking a significant shift toward organized play. The game quickly spread across Canada and into the United States, where it was embraced by educational institutions. Colleges and universities began to form teams, leading to the establishment of intercollegiate competition and the creation of governing bodies to oversee the sport.

By the early 20th century, lacrosse had evolved into two primary forms: field lacrosse and box lacrosse. Field lacrosse, played outdoors on a large field, became the standard version of the sport, particularly in the United States and Canada. Meanwhile, box lacrosse emerged as a popular indoor variant, gaining traction in Canada. This version of the game is played in a smaller, enclosed space and has its own distinct set of rules, contributing to the sport's growth and popularity in urban areas.

Today, lacrosse is recognized as one of the fastest-growing sports in North America. It has expanded internationally, with leagues and teams forming in Europe, Asia, and Australia. The sport has also gained recognition at the collegiate and professional levels, with major leagues such as Major League Lacrosse (MLL) and the Premier Lacrosse League (PLL) showcasing top talent. Lacrosse's continued evolution reflects its deep cultural roots and its ability to adapt and thrive in various settings, making it a unique and enduring part of sporting history.
